squatting

Squatting epitomises the DiY ethos. In fact, many aspects of what is now DiY stemmed from the exstensive squatting movement of the 1970's. It's emphasis on personal creativity, freedom from the repression of bureaucracy and it's overt anti-capitalist philosophy made it the perfect breeding ground for a 1990's counterculture intent on doing things it's own way.

The squatting movement has always been a melting pot for countercultural ideas. Post-Situationist groups like King Mob and the enteragé surrounding Malcom McLarren had their feet firmly fixed in the London squatting movement.

Contrary to popular belief, squatters are not all middle class drop-outs. Squatting exists as a social phenomenon as one answer to the problem of urban homelessness.

Probably because of it's nature as a quasi-legal way of life, it has inevitably evolved many countercultural facets. The Brighton based Anarchist Teapot are a prime example; serving up free tea and anarchy to the local community.

Squatting questions the nature of property and capital bondage. What better way to break away from production time and consumption time and discover the value of free time.
